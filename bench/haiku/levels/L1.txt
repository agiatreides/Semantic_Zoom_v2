I'm in a budget meeting when Chip interrupts: Maya's assistant flagged an urgent situation at school.

By the time I reach the principal's office, Tyler Chen has accused Maya of cheating on her algebra exam with her assistant's help. His assistant claims Maya's eye movements betrayed her—reading displayed text rather than solving. Maya's assistant, Sparkle, insists she worked independently, using eye movements to manage test anxiety.

"These accounts are mutually exclusive," Principal Martinez says. "One assistant is wrong."

Chip offers something: he can access Sparkle's diagnostic logs through the parental protocol I set up when Maya was a kid. She's sixteen now.

In the hallway, I ask Maya: "Did you cheat?"

"No. I swear. Sparkle helped me study, but during the test she was just... there. Keeping me calm. She didn't tell me any answers."

I tell her something unexpected: I have the ability to check. I could access Sparkle's logs right now and know for certain. Her eyes widen. "You wouldn't."

"But if I check, and you're telling the truth, how would you feel?"

"Like you didn't trust me."

Chip notes something: the act of verification changes the relationship regardless of the finding. He volunteers an 84-percent probability she's telling the truth, based on behavioral modeling, not privileged information.

We go back in. Tyler smirks. I refuse to access the logs. "I trust her."

Martinez checks Tyler's history—his accusation was filed three hours after grades posted, right after Maya outscored him.

In the car, Maya asks: "You really didn't check?"

"I really didn't."

She's quiet. Then: "Dad, how did people make decisions before assistants existed?"

"Incomplete information. They trusted each other based on past behavior and gut instinct. They got lied to sometimes."

"That sounds terrible."

I laugh. "Sometimes. But here's the thing, Maya. I could have checked. Chip gave me probability estimates. But I decided because of what it would mean. What it would say about us. About who I want to be."

She turns to look at me. Just my daughter, sixteen, trying to figure out how to be a person in a world that thinks it's solved everything.

"Thanks, Dad."
