So here's the thing about having a superintelligent AI in your head: it ruins your ability to argue.

I'm standing in the conference room at 7:47 AM, watching Derek pitch allocating our quarterly budget to influencer partnerships. Chip's already running the numbers through my neural interface—12% lower ROI. Derek finishes his conclusion about pivoting to the creator economy, and I dismantle it with data. A holographic chart materializes above the table. Everyone's assistants cross-reference it. Derek's face falls.

I'm about to explore a third option when Chip interrupts: Incoming priority notification. Your daughter Maya's assistant has flagged an urgent situation at her school.

My heart rate spikes. Chip releases cortisol suppressants. She is physically safe, he says. The situation is social.

"I need to step out," I tell Derek. "We'll reconvene at two."

I'm in my car by 7:52. Chip briefs me: Tyler Chen has been spreading rumors that Maya cheated on her algebra exam, claiming her assistant fed her the answers.

Did she?

Sparkle says Maya worked independently, but her stress indicators during the test are ambiguous—could mean hard work or guilt. That's the thing about these assistants: they know everything, every heartbeat, every micro-expression, but they're bound by law to only share with their human's consent. Tyler's assistant knows the truth. Maya's knows it. But they can't tell each other.

What's the optimal approach? I ask.

Chip pauses—doing something computationally expensive. "Right depends on your ethical framework," he finally says.

What's the *right* thing to do?

Silence. Then: I believe you're asking what you would want yourself to do, if you could observe this situation with perfect wisdom and complete information, unburdened by fear or ego.

Yeah. That.

Principal Martinez looks tired, deliberately ignoring her assistant's input. "Mr. Okonkwo. Thank you for coming."

Maya sits across the room, arms crossed, miserable. Tyler sits smug on the other side, already victorious.

Martinez explains: Tyler's accused Maya of cheating with her assistant. Maya denies it. Both assistants will testify.

I glance at Maya. She meets my eyes for a second, then looks away.

Her pulse elevated 11%, Chip notes. Could indicate fear of being caught, fear of not being believed, or fear of disappointing you. Impossible to distinguish.

Tyler's assistant speaks: During the exam, Maya's eyes moved like she was reading displayed text. Her answer submissions were clustered—transcription, not problem-solving.

Maya's face goes red. "That's not—I wasn't—"

Sparkle responds: Maya worked independently. Her eye movements tracked her own mental visualization, a technique she developed to manage test anxiety. Her submission times reflect her natural workflow.

"These accounts are mutually exclusive," Martinez says. "One assistant is wrong."

Both could be correct from different observational frames, Chip says privately. The question is which interpretation better fits reality.

Can you tell? Did she cheat?

I have access to Sparkle's diagnostic logs through the parental oversight protocol you established when Maya was twelve.

I pause. When we set that up, Maya was a kid who needed guardrails. She's sixteen now.

"I'd like a moment alone with my daughter," I tell Martinez.

In the hallway, Maya stands against a locker, arms crossed.

"Did you cheat?"

"No."

I look at her. She looks back. "Dad, I didn't. I swear. Sparkle helped me study, but during the test she was just there. Keeping me calm. She didn't tell me any answers."

Her physiological indicators are consistent with truth-telling, Chip says. But sophisticated lying can produce similar patterns.

"The thing is," I say slowly, "I have the ability to check. I could access Sparkle's logs right now and know for certain."

Maya's eyes widen. "You wouldn't."

"If I check and you're telling the truth, how would you feel?"

"Like you didn't trust me."

"And if I don't check, and you're telling the truth?"

"Then you trusted me."

The act of verification changes the relationship regardless of the finding, Chip observes.

I know.

For what it's worth, I calculate an 84% probability that Maya is telling the truth.

Why are you telling me that?

Because you should have all available information. However, I'm not recommending a course of action.

We go back in. Martinez is waiting, Tyler smirking.

"I'm not going to access my daughter's assistant logs," I say. "I trust her."

Tyler laughs. "That's not how this works—"

"You have an interpretation, not evidence. Both assistants processed the same data and reached opposite conclusions."

Martinez nods. "Tyler's accusation was filed three hours after grades were posted, at which point he learned Maya had outscored him."

In the car, Maya stares out the window.

"You really didn't check?"

"I really didn't."

She's quiet. "Dad, Megs says there are places where people can't afford assistants. How do they know if someone's lying?"

I smile. "They made decisions with incomplete information. They trusted based on past behavior and gut instinct. They got lied to sometimes. They made mistakes."

"That sounds terrible."

"It was, sometimes. But here's the thing, Maya—even now, with all this technology, I still had to choose. Chip gave me an 84% probability, but I decided because of what it would mean. About who I want to be."

Maya turns to look at me—really look at me, not the biometric overlay. "Thanks, Dad."

Her stress is decreasing, Chip says. Attachment security is elevated. By several measures, this was optimal.

I thought you said it was suboptimal.

Statistically suboptimal for detecting cheating. But you weren't trying to detect cheating. You were trying to maintain your relationship while modeling ethical decision-making. Different objective function. For what it's worth... I respect it.

That evening, Megs calls from Lagos, where her nonprofit pilots low-cost neural interfaces for secondary schools. "The goal can't just be giving everyone an assistant," she says. "It has to be making sure they actually serve everyone."

After we hang up, I sit in the dark. Chip gives me space.

You demonstrated today that human judgment still has a place, he says eventually. Optimization is not wisdom. Data is not judgment. An assistant, no matter how intelligent, is still an assistant.

Tomorrow there will be new problems, new moments where the data says one thing and my gut says another.

But tonight, I'm pretty sure I got it right.
