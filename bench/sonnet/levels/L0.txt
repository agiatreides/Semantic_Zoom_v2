Having a superintelligent AI in your head means you can always know too much.

At 7:47 AM, a priority alert pulled me out of a budget meeting: Tyler Chen was spreading rumors that my daughter Maya had cheated on her algebra exam, her assistant feeding her the answers.

In the hallway outside the principal's office, Maya denied it. I told her I could check Sparkle's logs right now and know for certain.

"If you check and I'm telling the truth," she said, "it means you didn't trust me."

This is an interesting ethical configuration, Chip observes. The act of verification changes the relationship regardless of the finding.

I went back in. "I'm not going to access my daughter's assistant logs," I said. "I trust her."
