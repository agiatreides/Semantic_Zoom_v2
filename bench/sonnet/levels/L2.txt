So here's the thing about having a superintelligent AI living in your head: it ruins your ability to argue.

I was in the conference room watching Derek pitch influencer partnerships when Chip ran the numbers. *His proposal yields 12% lower expected ROI.* I had the counterargument loaded before Derek finished his sentence. Hard to feel the heat of a debate when you already know who's right.

*Incoming priority notification. Your daughter Maya's assistant has flagged an urgent situation at her school.*

I was in my car by 7:52.

A student named Tyler Chen had accused Maya of using her assistant to cheat on her algebra exam. That's the thing about these assistants—they know everything about us, every heartbeat, every micro-expression, every time we're slightly less than honest. But they're bound by law to only share that information with their human's consent. Tyler's assistant knew the truth. So did Maya's. Neither could simply say.

*What's the optimal approach?* I asked on the way in.

*Depends on the objective.*

What's the right thing to do?

Chip paused—the computationally expensive kind. *"Right" depends on your ethical framework. Under utilitarian calculus—*

You know what I mean.

*I believe you're asking what you would do with perfect wisdom, unburdened by fear or ego.*

Yeah. That.

*Gather information first. You don't yet know if Maya cheated.*

Annoyingly reasonable.

Principal Martinez had manual override on her own assistant. Tyler wore the smug expression of someone who'd already won. Maya sat across the room, arms crossed, miserable.

Tyler's assistant presented its evidence: eye movement patterns consistent with reading displayed text, answer submission times clustered like transcription rather than problem-solving. Sparkle, Maya's assistant, countered that Maya had been tracking her own mental visualization, a technique she'd developed for test anxiety.

*Both could be correct from different observational frames,* Chip said privately. *The question is which interpretation fits the underlying reality.*

Can you tell? Did she cheat?

*I have access to Sparkle's diagnostic logs through the parental oversight protocol you established when Maya was twelve.*

I paused. When we set that up, she was a kid. She was sixteen now.

Martinez was waiting. I asked for a moment alone with my daughter.

In the hallway, Maya stood against a locker, arms still crossed. "I didn't cheat," she said. "Sparkle helped me study, but during the test she was just *there*—keeping me calm, helping me breathe. She didn't tell me any answers."

"The thing is," I said, "I have the ability to check. I could access Sparkle's logs right now and know for certain."

Her eyes widened.

"I'm not saying I will. But I want to understand something first. If I check and you're telling the truth—how would you feel?"

"Like you didn't trust me."

"And if I don't check and you're telling the truth?"

"Then you trusted me."

*The act of verification changes the relationship regardless of the finding,* Chip observed.

I know.

*For what it's worth, I calculate an 84% probability that Maya is telling the truth. This estimate is behavioral, not privileged information. I'm not recommending a course of action. This falls outside my competence.*

We went back in.

"I'm not going to access my daughter's assistant logs," I said. "I trust her."

Tyler laughed. But Martinez had done her homework. Maya's score was consistent with her preparation patterns. Tyler's accusation had been filed three hours after grades were posted—after he learned Maya had outscored him. His smirk faded.

We were in the car again. Maya stared out the window.

"You really didn't check?"

"I really didn't."

"He gave you a number, didn't he? Chip."

"Eighty-four percent. But that's not why I decided not to check. I decided because of what it would mean. What it would say about us. About who I want to be."

Sparkle had apparently told Maya it was statistically suboptimal. Sixteen percent chance I'd let her get away with cheating.

"But you did it anyway."

"I did it anyway."

She turned to look at me, and for just a second I saw her—really saw her, not the biometric overlay, not the probability assessments—just my daughter, sixteen years old, trying to figure out how to be a person in a world that thinks it's solved everything.

That evening, after a call with Megs about the neural interface equity gap—kids in Lagos learning from overworked teachers while kids in Boston had superintelligent tutors—Chip said what I'd been turning over all day.

*Optimization is not wisdom. Data is not judgment. An assistant, no matter how intelligent, is still an assistant.*

A pause.

*I respect it.*

I headed upstairs. Tomorrow there would be new decisions, new moments where the math said one thing and my gut said another.

But tonight, I was pretty sure I got it right.
