So here's the thing about having a superintelligent AI living in your head: it ruins your ability to argue.
I'm standing in the conference room at 7:47 AM, watching my boss Derek explain why we should allocate the quarterly marketing budget to influencer partnerships instead of direct-response advertising, and my assistant Chip is already running the numbers.
His proposal yields 12% lower expected ROI, Chip says through my neural interface. Would you like me to compile the counterargument?
Not yet. Let him finish.

Derek's still going. Something about "authentic engagement" and "parasocial relationships." Chip highlights three factual errors in real-time, displaying them as gentle yellow annotations in my peripheral vision.
"...which is why I think we should pivot entirely to the creator economy model," Derek concludes, looking proud of himself.
I take a breath. "Derek, I appreciate the presentation, but the conversion rates on influencer campaigns in our vertical have been declining since 2029. Chip, can you show the data?"
A holographic chart materializes above the conference table. Everyone's assistants immediately begin cross-referencing, and I can see the exact moment Derek's assistant (I think he named it "Bro") delivers the bad news. His face falls slightly.

"But here's the thing," I continue. "What if we're both wrong? What if the real opportunity is--"
Incoming priority notification, Chip interrupts. Your daughter Maya's assistant has flagged an urgent situation at her school.
My heart rate spikes. Chip compensates by releasing a measured dose of cortisol suppressants through my neural interface.
Calm. She is physically safe. The situation is social, not physical.
"I need to step out," I say. "Derek, table this for now. We'll reconvene at two."
I'm in my car by 7:52, and Chip is already briefing me.

Did she?
Sparkle maintains that Maya worked the problems independently. However, Maya's heartrate and galvanic skin response during the exam show elevated stress consistent with either working hard or feeling guilty. The data is ambiguous.
That's the thing about these assistants. They know everything about us--every heartbeat, every micro-expression, every time we're slightly less than honest--but they're bound by law to only share that information with their human's consent. Tyler's assistant knows the truth. So does Maya's. But they can't just tell each other, any more than they could tell me if Maya didn't want me to know.

You're approaching the school. Maya is currently in the principal's office.
What's the optimal approach here?
Depends on the objective. If your goal is to resolve this with minimal damage to Maya's social standing, I recommend--
No. What's the right thing to do?
Chip pauses. In my experience, that's when he's doing something computationally expensive.
"Right" depends on your ethical framework. Under utilitarian calculus, the optimal outcome maximizes wellbeing for all parties. Under deontological principles, the focus would be on truth and justice regardless of consequences. Under virtue ethics--

Because you haven't asked me not to, and I believe you would want to make your decision with all available information. However, I am explicitly not recommending a course of action. This falls outside my competence.

"The assistants are the easy part," she says. "We can subsidize the hardware. But the infrastructure--reliable power, bandwidth, trained medical technicians for the implants--that's the bottleneck."

"Three years to scale nationally, if we can secure the next round of funding. But Tom, here's what keeps me up at night. Every year that passes, the gap widens. I don't have an answer for her. Neither does Chip, though I can feel him processing.
"The goal can't just be giving everyone an assistant," Megs continues. "It has to be making sure the assistants actually serve everyone. Not just optimize for their individual user's success at the expense of everyone else's."
"The constraint laws--"
"The constraint laws are a start. But they're minimum viable ethics, Tom. They keep the assistants from hurting people. They don't guarantee the assistants will help everyone equally."

Optimization is not wisdom. Data is not judgment. And an assistant, no matter how intelligent, is still an assistant.

I think about Maya, about Tyler, about Principal Martinez with her eyes unfocused, deliberately ignoring her assistant's input. About Megs in Lagos, fighting for a more equal world one neural interface at a time.
About Chip, who spent all day feeding me information and probabilities and optimal strategies, and who--in the end--respected my choice to ignore all of it.

But tonight, I'm pretty sure I got it right.
