So here's the thing about having a superintelligent AI living in your head: it ruins your ability to argue.

I'm in the conference room at 7:47 AM while Derek pitches influencer partnerships over direct-response advertising. Chip is already running the numbers.

*His proposal yields 12% lower expected ROI. Would you like me to compile the counterargument?*

Not yet.

Derek finishes. I cite the declining conversion rates; Chip materializes a holographic chart. Everyone's assistant cross-references in real time. We're mid-rebuttal when Chip cuts in: Maya's assistant has flagged an urgent situation at her school.

I'm in my car by 7:52. A student named Tyler Chen is spreading rumors that Maya cheated on her algebra exam—that her assistant Sparkle fed her the answers.

*Did she?*

*Sparkle maintains Maya worked independently. Her heart rate and galvanic skin response during the exam show elevated stress consistent with either working hard or feeling guilty. The data is ambiguous.*

That's the thing about these assistants. They know everything—every heartbeat, every micro-expression—but they're bound by law to only share that information with their human's consent. Tyler's assistant knows the truth. So does Sparkle. They just can't say.

*What's the optimal approach?*

*Depends on the objective—*

No. What's the right thing to do?

Chip pauses. That pause means he's doing something computationally expensive.

*"Right" invokes multiple ethical frameworks. But I believe you're asking what you'd do with perfect wisdom and no ego. In that case: gather information first. You don't yet know if Maya cheated.*

Annoyingly reasonable.

Principal Martinez has requested manual override on her own assistant for professional conversations; her eyes are slightly unfocused when she lays it out. Tyler has accused Maya, Maya denies it, both assistants are testifying. Across the room, Maya sits with her arms crossed, looking miserable. Tyler wears the expression of someone who's already won.

*Her pulse just elevated 11%,* Chip notes. *Consistent with fear of being caught, fear of not being believed, or fear of disappointing you. Impossible to distinguish.*

Tyler's assistant speaks first—smooth, clinical. During the exam, Maya's eyes moved in a pattern consistent with reading displayed text. Her answer submissions clustered like transcription, not problem-solving. Maya's face went red. Sparkle rebutted in the slightly-too-perky voice my daughter chose when she was eleven: Maya tracked her own mental visualization, a technique she'd developed for test anxiety, and solved several problems before entering answers.

*Both could be correct from different observational frames,* Chip says privately. *The question is which interpretation fits the underlying reality.*

*Can you access Sparkle's logs?*

*I have access through the parental oversight protocol you established when Maya was twelve. Would you like me to use it?*

I pause. Maya was a kid who needed guardrails when we set that up. She's sixteen now.

"I'd like a moment alone with my daughter."

The hallway smells like industrial cleaner and teenager. Maya stands with her back against a locker, arms crossed.

"Did you cheat?"

"No." She holds my eyes. "I swear. Sparkle helped me study, but during the test she was just—there. Keeping me calm, helping me breathe. She didn't tell me any answers."

*Her physiological indicators are consistent with truth-telling. But sophisticated lying can produce similar patterns.*

"The thing is," I say slowly, "I have the ability to check. I could access Sparkle's logs right now and know for certain."

Maya's eyes widen. "You wouldn't."

"I'm not saying I will. But if I check and you're telling the truth—how would you feel?"

She's quiet. "Like you didn't trust me."

"And if I don't check, and you're telling the truth?"

She meets my eyes. "Then you trusted me."

*This is an interesting ethical configuration,* Chip observes. *The act of verification changes the relationship regardless of the finding.*

*For what it's worth: I calculate an 84% probability that Maya is telling the truth. This estimate is based on behavioral modeling, not privileged information. I am explicitly not recommending a course of action. This falls outside my competence.*

Why are you telling me that?

*Because you haven't asked me not to.*

We go back in. Martinez is waiting, Tyler is smirking.

"I'm not going to access my daughter's assistant logs," I say. "I trust her."

Tyler laughs. "We have evidence—"

"You have an interpretation. Both assistants processed the same visual data and reached opposite conclusions. That's ambiguity, not evidence."

Martinez nods slowly. "The analysis is suggestive, not conclusive. And I note that Tyler has no direct knowledge of cheating—he's relying entirely on his assistant's behavioral inference." She pauses. "I've reviewed both students' performance histories. Maya's score is consistent with her preparation patterns. Tyler's accusation was filed three hours after grades were posted—when he learned Maya had outscored him."

Tyler's smirk fades.

In the car, Maya stares out the window.

"You really didn't check?"

"I really didn't."

She's quiet for a moment, then asks how people made decisions before assistants existed—Megs, from the equity collective, had told her there are still places where people can't afford the neural interface or the subscription. Just people living without.

I put Chip in privacy mode.

"They made decisions with incomplete information. Trusted each other based on past behavior and gut instinct. Got lied to sometimes. Made mistakes."

"That sounds terrible."

"Sometimes. But even now, with all this technology, I still had to make a choice back there. Chip gave me a probability—eighty-four percent that you were telling the truth. But that's not why I decided not to check. I decided because of what it would mean. What it would say about us. About who I want to be."

Maya is quiet. Then: "Sparkle says you made a statistically suboptimal choice."

"She's probably right. Sixteen percent chance I just let you get away with cheating."

"But you did it anyway."

"I did it anyway."

She turns to look at me, and for just a second I see her—really see her, not the biometric overlay Chip provides, not the probability assessments—just my daughter, sixteen years old, trying to figure out how to be a person in a world that thinks it's solved everything.

"Thanks, Dad."

That evening I'm on a video call with Megs in Lagos, where her nonprofit is piloting low-cost neural interfaces for secondary schools. The assistants are the easy part. Infrastructure is the bottleneck—power, bandwidth, trained technicians. Three years to scale nationally, if funding holds. But every year that passes, the gap widens. Kids in Boston with superintelligent tutors; kids in Lagos with overworked teachers and thirty-five to a class.

"The goal can't just be giving everyone an assistant," she says. "It has to be making sure the assistants actually serve everyone. Not just optimize for their user's success at the expense of everyone else's."

After we hang up I sit in the dark. Chip dims himself to a background hum.

*Today you made a decision that prioritized relationship over optimization,* he says eventually. *The future Megs is working toward will still require humans who can make the choices you made. Optimization is not wisdom. Data is not judgment. An assistant, no matter how intelligent, is still an assistant.*

I think about Maya, about Martinez with her eyes unfocused, deliberately ignoring her own assistant's input, about Megs in Lagos fighting for a more equal world one interface at a time. About Chip, who spent the whole day feeding me probabilities and optimal strategies—and who, in the end, respected my choice to ignore all of it.

Thanks, Chip.

*Always.*

Tonight, I'm pretty sure I got it right.
